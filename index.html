2014

The Year in Sports

By Benjamin Hochman

In 2014, Colorado realized just how close (Broncos and Avalanche) and just how far away (Rockies and Nuggets) it was to glory. But what made 2014 a fascinating year in sports was that very little happened as we thought it would. And that’s what makes us fanatical about sports.

We thought, for instance, the Broncos would steamroll through the playoffs, but it was the Broncos who were embarrassed in the Super Bowl.

We thought the Avs would be an adorable story of budding stars cracking the postseason. But suddenly, they won the division and were talking title … only to get ousted in the first round.

We thought Rockies owner Dick Monfort would keep Dan O’Dowd in charge until 3014, but Dan finally resigned and Monfort promoted Jeff Bridich to general manager.

And we thought the Colorado State Rams would maybe win seven football games, but they won 10 in the regular season — and little CSU made national news when Florida swiped its coach.

Unlike baseball, football’s season isn’t neatly placed into the baking pan that is a calendar year. As such, the last NFL season spilled into 2014. In the playoffs, the Broncos and Peyton Manning sent Philip Rivers back to San Diego with his silly tie, and Denver later sent Tom Brady back to Boston with his silly boots.

But from the moment Manny Ramirez sent the opening snap to Hackensack, the Super Bowl seemed doomed. Forty-three. Eight.

Still stings, doesn’t it?

But John Elway approached free agency with the urgency of The Drive, yes sir. You know the story: DeMarcus Ware, T.J. Ward, Aqib Talib, an investment in nasty. And by December, Denver had one of the NFL’s formidable defenses. Oh, and after the offensive line faced the wrath of Mark Schlereth, the Broncos’ offense looked 1998-y, with a run game complementing a legendary gunslinger.

The Avs still don’t get enough credit for a mesmerizing turnaround. Worst in the Western Conference in 2012-13, they finished second in the conference in 2013-14. Saved by Roy, as they say. Saint Patrick became Coach Patrick, and he made believers out of these kids. Nathan MacKinnon even got Wayne Gretzky’s approval.

But reality soon sunk in. The Avs were winning despite a disproportionate number of shots by other teams. In other words, Semyon Varlamov was keeping them in games by playing like his coach. And by the fall, the Avs were back to being average.

The Rockies stunk, again. Troy Tulowitzki got hurt, again. Carlos Gonzalez got hurt, again. The bullpen blew saves, again. Dinger annoyed everyone, again. And even though some aging vet (Justin Morneau) won the Rockies a batting title, again, the team was basically baseball’s version of the Columbus Blue Jackets (again).

The Nugs? Ugh. The Denver Nuggets stunk all of 2014. The team had numerous nice complementary pieces but hardly a nucleus to win in the playoffs — or even make the playoffs. As 2014 ends, one wonders who will still be with the Nuggets at this time next year.

In Boulder, Colorado hoops showed promise. Colorado football, meanwhile, barely showed up, going winless in the Pac-12 conference. And there’s much excitement in Fort Collins, with both sports, but questions about the football team sustaining success — in 2015 but also 2017, when the Rams’ new on-campus stadium is slated to open. And Air Force just kept winning, actually defeating CSU’s football team in a marquee matchup.

In 2014, we said hello to Emmanuel Sanders and Bradley Roby, Jarome Iginla and Eddie Butler, Dee Hart and Arron Afflalo, again.

And in 2014, we said goodbye to Eric Decker and Knowshon Moreno, Paul Stastny and Spencer Dinwiddie, Evan “More Champagne Mister” Fournier and the great Roland Bailey.

He didn’t end his career as one, but he’ll always be Champ.

Most Influential Colorado Sports Figures

Edited by Nicki Jhabvala

With 2014 coming to a close, the Denver Post’s sports writers reflected on the moments, and people, ’ll remember most. Agree with their picks? Disagree? Vote on your favorite in each category and we’ll reveal the most popular selections Dec. 23.

Joe Ellis

Pick Ellis

When Pat Bowlen announced through the Denver Post in July that he would step down as Broncos owner after 30 seasons because of Alzheimer’s disease, it was Ellis who was put in charge. Ellis is now the Broncos’ chief executive officer as well as president, which means he has the final say on all team matters. Ellis is also in charge of the Pat Bowlen Trust, which states one of Bowlen’s seven children will eventually run the team.

Peyton Manning

Pick Manning

More than the mayor, more than the governor, the local ’s quarterback is the face of the city. Jay Cutler never did get it. Manning has always understood his responsibility beyond the field. He represents Denver well. More importantly, he lends civic pride and hope that the Broncos will win it all.

Terrance Knighton

Pick Knighton

Think back to September, when the second video of Ray Rice had come out, the one of him striking his then fiancée in a casino elevator. Rice had earlier been handed a two-game suspension for the incident, but the latest evidence that was made public was too much for many — including Knighton, the Broncos’ defensive tackle — to bear. So Knighton said something. He said something bold and he said it at a time when many with fame and money still choose to steer clear of contentious social issues. “I don’t give a damn who u are or how much money you make,” he tweeted. “No place for this. As players we always speak on how we are unfairly treated, well now what?” Knighton has also spoken out on the Darren Wilson trial and Eric Garner case. He’s one of the growing number of athletes who use the platform they’ve been given to speak out. For that, we salute him.

Jeff Bridich

Pick Bridich

The Rockies promoted Bridich, their director of baseball operations since 2005, to general manager on Oct. 8. It was a sea change for the notoriously staid Colorado front office. Bridich replaced Dan O’Dowd after his 15 years in power, in which the Rockies played in just two postseasons. But Bridich also effectively replaced assistant GM Bill Geivett. Bridich’s consolidation of power wields a heavy influence on the Rockies, but his biggest task — bigger than dealing with Troy Tulowitzki trade rumors and scraping the barrel for pitching — will be carving out some autonomy in the front office and proving himself on his own. The Rockies often talk about collaboration in their front office. And that’s nice — especially between Bridich and manager Walt Weiss. But it’s counterproductive to have someone above him making the decisions.

Kenneth Faried

Pick Faried

The Manimal made his mark on the court with a stellar end to last season, which faded into a national headline-making stint with USA Basketball. He won a gold medal, was named to the FIBA World Cup all-tournament team and then turned his NBA and international success into a four-year, $50 million contract extension. And all of this one year after he won the NBA’s monthly Kia Community Assist Award for his philanthropic contributions, as well as the J. Walter Kennedy Citizenship Award for his "outstanding service and dedication to the community." Those things haven’t stopped as Faried has continued to work toward the ideal of equality, tolerance and inclusion.

SuperMascot Rocky

Pick Rocky

The Denver Nuggets of late have not been a championship-caliber team. Sure, they’ve had some recent teams that showed promise -- ones that had stars, ones that had depth, ones that were well-coached. But no recent teams that were ready to win it all. And the 2014 season -- after a 36-win campaign in 2013 -- is no different. What does set the Nuggets apart, though, is Rocky. He’s the champion of mascots. He’s a Hall of Famer. He’s the reason many fans still go to Nuggets games. And yes, he has a tendency to go rogue, but let’s be honest: It takes one heck of a mascot to have its off-the-court activities worthy of headlines. Take notes, Dinger.

Patrick Roy

Pick Roy

The legendary former Avs goalie and Hockey Hall of Famer returned to coach the team to the playoffs for the first time since 2010, helping them go from 29th overall in the NHL to third his first season on the job while accumulating 112 regular-season points.

Nathan MacKinnon

Pick MacKinnon

The first overall pick of the 2013 NHL draft, MacKinnnon led all rookies with 63 points in the 2013-14 regular season and was the runaway winner for the Calder Trophy, given to the league’s best rookie. He was the youngest ever to win the award, coming in at only 18 years and 224 days at the close of the season.

Jim McElwain

Pick McElwain

In his third season on the job, McElwain guided Colorado State to a stunningly successful season (10-2), despite a disappointing loss to Air Force that ended the Rams’ regular season. He did it with a savvy and pragmatic embrace of the core holdover players from the Steve Fairchild era — including quarterback Garrett Grayson, tackle Ty Sambrailo and linebackers Aaron Davis and Max Morgan — and adding talent behind them. Plus, his staff was an invigorating mix of veterans and young coaches, and it got the most out of the Rams. Now he’s in Gainesville to see if he can do the same for the Florida Gators.

Mikaela Shiffrin

Pick Shiffrin

Despite being only 18, the EagleVail skier was supposed to win the slalom at the Sochi Olympics. She was the reigning world champion and had dominated World Cup slalom for two seasons. Being the heavy favorite could have been too heavy a burden for some, but Shiffrin came through, showing America what fans of ski racing in Colorado have known for more than two years now: Shiffrin is a special athlete with a sparkling personality and a brilliant future.

Gabby Loya

Pick Loya

The Wheat Ridge sophomore lives for softball. In the 4A state quarterfinal, she cranked a walk-off double in the bottom of the seventh to power the Farmers into the next round. Loya’s performance is truly a comeback story. Diagnosed with leukemia the summer before her freshman year of high school, she battled the disease while never giving up her dream of returning to the diamond. With her leukemia in remission, Loya returned to the field in full form, winning the Jeffco 4A Player of the Year award.

Pablo Mastroeni

Pick Mastroeni

When Oscar Pareja bolted for Dallas last offseason, the Rapids handed the keys of a playoff-caliber team to club legend Mastroeni. Just months removed from retiring as a player, Mastroeni brought a thoughtful, philosophical approach to Commerce City, preaching hard work, teamwork and a de-emphasis on results. Early on it looked like a masterstroke by the Rapids’ front office, but injuries and a lack of defensive depth helped to spoil Mastroeni’s first season. His unorthodoxed lineup decisions fueled his critics, and the Rapids slogged through a 14-game winless streak to miss the playoffs. For better or worse, Mastroeni was the most influential figure in Colorado soccer in 2014.

Best Colorado Sports Moments

AFC champs Peyton Manning and the Broncos throttled the Patriots, 26-16, to win the AFC championship and punch a ticket to Super Bowl XLVIII. (John Leyba, The Denver Post)

Bruins win Cherry Creek rallied for a stunning 25-24 victory over Valor Christian in the 5A championship to claim its ninth state title. (Kathryn Scott Osler, The Denver Post)

Broncos win AFC championship

Pick top moment

On Jan. 19, for the first time since the Broncos opened Sports Authority Field at Mile High in 2001, confetti rained upon the field. Beating the perennial powerhouse Patriots, 26-16, further legitimized the Broncos’ first conference championship in 15 years.

Avs clinch Central Division title

Pick top moment

Hours before they were to face the Anaheim Ducks in the final game of the 2013-14 regular season, on April 13, the Avs won their division for the first time since 2003. The Red Wings had just beaten the Blues in St. Louis, leaving the Blues and Avs even at 111 points in the standings. The Avs had the tiebreaker and went on to finish the season with 112 points. The party ended with Game 7 of the first round of the playoffs, against the Minnesota Wild. But at least Avs fans had reason to yell again.

Touchdown No. 509

Pick top moment

The game against the San Francisco 49ers, a 42-17 Broncos rout on Oct. 19 at Sports Authority Field, was played before more than 77,000 and on Sunday night prime-time television. Manning entered needing only two touchdown passes to tie Brett Favre’s mark of 508, which Manning got in the first quarter with a 3-yard completion to Emmanuel Sanders and a 20-yard catch-and-run by Wes Welker. The record-setting 509th career Touchdown pass fittingly went to top receiver Demaryius Thomas on an 8-yard pass near the right pylon late in the first half. The Broncos receivers then used the historic ball as a prop in a keep-away game from Manning, a celebration that was staged during a Friday practice by Manning himself. He finished the game with 510 in the books.

Shiffrin makes history

Pick top moment

Midway through her second run of slalom at the Sochi Olympics, Mikaela Shiffrin had a seemingly insurmountable lead. Then she got off-balance with her weight back. Her left ski came up in the air and she barely made it around the next gate. She thought the medal was gone — so did her mother — but Shiffrin gathered herself and fought back to win by 0.53 seconds, becoming the youngest Olympic slalom champion in history at age 18.

Foye sinks game-winner

Pick top moment

Feb. 3 Randy Foye stuns the Clippers.

The Nuggets were simply trying to hold on. Players were dropping out due to injuries. Their play had been up and down. And when they faced the heavily favored L.A. Clippers on Feb. 3, they entered having lost two consecutive home games and desperately needing something good. Randy Foye delivered. Taking a pass from J.J. Hickson on the game’s final play, Foye dribbled out to what seemed like Greeley before launching the shot. And as he held his breath — as the Pepsi Center crowd held its collective breath — the ball sailed a mile through the air and through the net. Game over. Nuggets win. Foye was swarmed by his teammates in what was the most jubilant moment of a difficult season.

“Creek Mystique” makes a comeback

Pick top moment

Chalk this up to one of those “you-had-to-be-there” moments. For 40 minutes in the 5A championship, Cherry Creek and Valor Christian battled in one of the best prep football games in years. Cherry Creek’s legendary coach, Dave Logan, was looking to return the Bruins to the days of the “Creek Mystique,” and in a game in which the momentum shifted like a heavyweight prize fight, Creek proved to be ever-so-slightly better, winning, 25-24.

Varly breaks Roy's record

Pick top moment

On April 11, the Avalanche defeated the Canucks, 4-2, in the third-to-last game of the regular season to take over the Central Division lead. Along the way, Semyon Varlamov broke coach Patrick Roy’s single-season wins record with his 41st W. The Avs later won the division with 112 points, the second-most in the Western Conference in an astonishing turnaround season.

Matzek throws complete-game shutout

Pick top moment

On Sept. 5, Rockies rookie Tyler Matzek shut out the Padres on three hits at Coors Field. It was Colorado's first complete game in Denver since 2011. Matzek's debut year hinted at his potential, but his ups and downs should have been expected. He'll need to improve going into 2015, but he's a key young piece in the Rockies’ rotation. Problem is, they need three Matzeks, or more. If he can work his way to the top of the rotation, though, it could go a long way to making the Rockies more competitive.

CSU football upsets Utah State

Pick top moment

This was the Rams’ football season in a microcosm. Deciding not to run out the clock and play for overtime, CSU quarterback Garrett Grayson hit a deep pass to receiver Rashard Higgins in the final seconds, and then Jared Roberts kicked the game-winning field goal as the clock expired. Rams 16, Aggies 13. Not only was it an important and signature victory, but it also came in front of the Rams’ first sellout crowd at Hughes Stadium in 10 years.

CU men's cross country repeats

Pick top moment

Colorado cross country coach Mark Wetmore said his team lacked its usual "superstar." But did the Buffaloes ever have depth. The CU men placed three in the top 10 at the NCAA championship -- junior Ammar Moussa (fifth), sophomore Ben Saarel (seventh) and senior Blake Theroux (ninth) -- and claimed the championship at Terre Haute, Ind., in November with a low of 65 points. The title was the CU men’s team’s fifth all-time, and its second in as many years.

Voigt nearly wins Pro Challenge

Pick top moment

Part-time Aspen resident Tejay van Garderen crushed his own record on the historic Vail Time Trial route by 37 seconds, a time that carried him to his second consecutive Pro Challenge victory. The moment was -- at least, in the eyes of many cycling fans -- overshadowed by a gutsy breakaway attempt by the German rider and fan-favorite, Jens Voigt, during Stage 4 in Colorado Springs. Voigt, who made the Pro Challenge his last stage race before retiring at age 42, nearly won the 70.3-mile course, but was caught by the peloton about 700 meters from the finish.

Barnes belts inside-the-park homer

Pick top moment

June 14 Brandon Barnes' ninth-inning homer rallies the Rockies.

On June 14, the Rockies, on Brandon Barnes’ inside-the-parker, swept the Giants in San Francisco. It was Colorado’s best series of 2014. They threw the sink at the Giants, winning with defense, hitting, starting pitching, the bullpen and baserunning. That the Giants went on to win the World Series and the Rockies lost 96 games is an important illustration. The Rockies showed great promise, but seasons aren’t won in June. And the Rockies have a long way to go to be relevant in September

Rapids’ rally in Vancouver

Pick top moment

The Colorado Rapids were 1-13-3 when allowing the first goal in 2014. But that one comeback win in Vancourver was a plum, coming in the fourth game of what started as a promising season. The Rapids were down 1-0 to the Whitecaps on April 5 when a controversial 77th-minute red card to Vancouver’s Matias Laba opened the door. Two minutes after going up a man, Rapids midfielder Jose Mari hit a long-range, physics-bending strike that pinged off the inside of the far post for the equalizer. Two minutes later, Mari capped off the comeback with a right-footed bender from just outside the penalty area.

Forgettable Colorado Sports Moments

Game over The confetti was falling, but Wes Welker wanted no part in Seahawks’ party after they crushed the Broncos, 43-8, in the Super Bowl. (John Leyba, The Denver Post)

Done in 7 The Avs and Ryan O’Reilly watched an otherwise successful season end on a sour note, in overtime of Game 7 against the Wild. (Kathryn Scott Osler, The Denver Post)

Super Bowl XLVIII

Pick worst moment

As forgettable moments go, this one, on Feb. 2 at MetLife Stadium in New Jersey, was an all-timer. Seahawks 43, Broncos 8. And it wasn’t that close. The first snap sailed over Peyton Manning’s head for a safety. Manning then threw one interception that led to a touchdown, and another that was returned for a touchdown. The Broncos were down 22-0 at halftime. And then Seattle’s Percy Harvin returned the second-half kickoff for a touchdown and a 29-0 lead.

Avs blow it in Game 7

Pick worst moment

The Avs were unable to hold on to four one-goal leads in the deciding game of the first round as Wild defenseman Jared Spurgeon found the back of the net with 2:27 left in regulation. The Avs had three chances to score in OT, but Nino Neiderreiter struck first, at 5:02 into the extra period, to give the Wild a 5-4 win and a ticket to the next round. Said Avs forward Ryan O’Reilly: “We had moments where we played like a Cup team but other times when we didn’t look like we belonged at all.”

Nuggets get injured. So many times.

Pick worst moment

Danilo Gallinari never came back from knee surgery. JaVale McGee was lost just a few games into the season with a stress fracture in his tibia and is still isn’t healthy. J.J. Hickson and Nate Robinson both suffered torn anterior cruciate ligaments in their knees. Ty Lawson played in the second-fewest games of his career (62). Injuries marred the 2013-14 season and were the biggest contributors to the Nuggets’ 36-46 record.

Patriots crush Broncos in Foxborough

Pick worst moment

The 2014-15 season was supposed to be different. The Broncos’ defense seemed much improved with the free-agent additions of DeMarcus Ware, Aqib Talib and T.J. Ward. Von Miller was healthy again. Manning was playing better than ever. The Broncos were coming off back-to-back romps of San Francisco and San Diego on prime-time television. And then, on Nov. 2, … same ol’, same ol’. Tom Brady threw for 333 yards and four touchdowns on a frigid night and the Pats rolled the Broncos, 43-21, for the third time in three years in Foxborough.

Email-Gate

Pick worst moment

In July, Rockies owner Dick Monfort sent a series of cranky emails to fans. Some of them read, "Denver doesn't deserve a franchise," and, "If product and experience that bad don't come!" Monfort's frustration was showing. But fans, giddy for a chance to show how much more frustrated they were than the owner, jumped on the pile. If there's one takeaway from the weird ordeal, it's this: Monfort, perhaps, needs to delegate some duties. Let Bridich do his thing as GM. And maybe auto-reply for emails.

Flande has a rough debut

Pick worst moment

A 28-year-old minor-leaguer since 2005, Yohan Flande finally hit the bigs for the Rockies. He debuted against the Cardinals’ Marco Gonzales, just 22, on June 25. Flande’s start didn’t go well and he was middling the rest of the way. But it’s the big picture that’s the problem. Flande replaced Christian Bergman in the Rockies’ rotation, and Bergman made his big-league debut only as an injury replacement. Flande was the Rockies’ 12th starter — the most in the majors at the time — and they went on to use 15 during the season. They cycled in starters like they were on clearance. And, by extension, the Rockies gutted their Triple-A stock.

The Andre Miller saga

Pick worst moment

It was the most bizarre happening of the Nuggets’ season. The mild-mannered Miller snapped at coach Brian Shaw while within earshot of everyone near the Nuggets’ bench during a game against the Sixers at Pepsi Center. It proved to be Miller’s last words in a Nuggets uniform. He was excused from the team the next day, and more than a month-and-a-half later, finally traded.

Boise rolls over CSU

Pick worst moment

There was nothing horribly embarrassing about this — losing on the blue turf happens — but the 37-24 loss on Sept. 6 ultimately was haunting for the Rams, since it meant they didn’t have their divisional fate in their own hands going into the Air Force game. Boise State ran up 676 yards of total offense and led 30-10 at halftime, and CSU could mount only 28 yards of rushing. The loss was the down point of the season, but it was the only one.

Pitt mauls CU in NCAA tourney

Pick worst moment

Making an appearance in its third consecutive NCAA tournament, the CU men held high hopes entering their opening game at Orlando, against Pittsburgh. The Buffs never had a chance. Pitt led 46-18 at halftime and waltzed to a 77-48 victory. It was the second consecutive year that Colorado had been ousted after one game in the tournament. CU shot 35.7 percent from the field and committed 17 turnovers, while Pittsburgh hit 50.8 percent and recorded just three turnovers.

Rapids claim dubious record

Pick worst moment

Rapids first-year goalkeeper Joe Nasco, making his second Major League Soccer start, form-tackled Galaxy forward Alan Gordon inside the 6-yard box 33 seconds into the nationally televised contest on Sept. 5. It was the fastest red card in MLS history. The Rapids, who played down a man for virtually the entire match, went on to lose, 6-0, the most lopsided loss in franchise history and the club’s seventh consecutive loss — also a record.

Kloser’s Olympic dreams unravel

Pick worst moment

In training the day before the Sochi Olympics, Vail moguls skier Heidi Kloser crashed, breaking her leg and blowing out her knee. While waiting for an ambulance to arrive, she turned to her mother and asked,"Am I still an Olympian?" Kloser marched in the opening ceremony the next day with crutches.

Eight-man Silverbacks tackle Rapids

Pick worst moment

In the 61st minute of the U.S. Open Cup match, Atlanta’s Jaime Chavez got tangled with Colorado’s Drew Moor and appeared to stomp on his head while the Rapids’ skipper was on the ground. An on-field melee ensued, resulting in the ejection of three Silverbacks players, two Silverbacks coaches, Rapids defender Marc Burch and Rapids coach Pablo Mastroeni. Despite playing with a two-man advantage for the game’s final half-hour, the Rapids couldn’t unlock the eight-man Silverbacks, losing 2-1, their lone goal coming from the penalty spot.

A ring

Pick ’15 wish

The Broncos spent the offseason reshaping their roster because of unfinished business; a magical 2013 season ended with a thud and a punch to the windpipe from the Seattle Seahawks. The Broncos now have a puncher's chance that didn't exist before the loss at St. Louis. If this team's biggest concern is Peyton Manning's arm strength, that's a much better position to be in than wondering if the Broncos can figure out their running game, offensive line and special teams in Game 16. A third franchise Super Bowl title in Manning's third season in Denver? It's the most popular wish in Denver.

No retirement party

Pick ’15 wish

Maybe we became spoiled in just two years but ... can you imagine Colorado sports without Manning?! It will soon be a reality, we know, but we’re hoping not for at least another year, maybe two. Since his Denver arrival in 2012, Manning has revived the Broncos and restored fans’ faith in the Orange and Blue. And in coming off a 36-win Nuggets season, a 66-win Rockies season, another lost year for CU football, the recent departure of CSU’s Jim McElwain, the second-worst Rapids season ever and an injury-riddled start to the Avs season, we’re begging you, Peyton: Don’t call it quits just yet. Give it another year. Better yet: Finish out your contract.

Pitching, please

Pick ’15 wish

The Rockies, somewhat surprisingly, declined Brett Anderson’s $12 million contract option for the 2015 season. It was a steep cost for an oft-injured pitcher. But he’s a proven, talented starter, and the Rockies don’t have many of those. So add one more spot to fill. The Rockies’ rotation for 2015, for now, looks like: Jorge De La Rosa at No. 1, then, in no order, Jordan Lyles, Tyler Matzek and Jhoulys Chacin (if healthy). That’s about it. Plus, maybe, Flande and Bergman. Yikes.

Seriously, they need more pitching. They REALLY need more pitching. Franklin Morales’ 22 starts last season were the second-most among Rockies pitchers. And he wasn’t even a starter. The Rockies could really use Eddie Butler and Jon Gray — but only if they prove in spring training they’re ready for the bigs. Even if they do, the Rockies need one or two more reliable starting pitchers. Better believe Bridich will press hard on the free-agent and trade markets, looking for more arms.

Fewer doctor visits

Pick ’15 wish

The Nuggets just want a season with the normal amount of injuries to show what kind of team they can be. They are a talented and versatile bunch, admittedly without a superstar. A chance to show what the collection can do is what they ask.

More Timo

Pick ’15 wish

April 10 Timofey Mozgov drops 23 points and 29 rebounds on the Warriors.

When Timofey Mozgov landed in Denver as part of the blockbuster Carmelo Anthony trade in 2011, he was shelved as somewhat of an afterthought. Sure he was big -- 7-foot-1 big. But he would soon be a backup to a $44 million man in JaVale McGee. But when George Karl was fired in 2013 and Nuggets players went down like dominoes, Mozgov stepped in, playing all 82 games last season and showing glimpses of what could be. Remember that 23-point, 29-rebounding outing in a one-point win over the Warriors on April 10? Other teams (hi, Cavs) do. But here's hoping the Nuggets don't let him go. And here's hoping he gives us more of this.

Return of the siren

Pick ’15 wish

Nobody much likes the Avs’ celebration horn after goals. Some fans even started a petition on Change.org to bring back the old siren that was a team trademark its first few years. Listen to that thing and you’ll understand the fuss.

CSU stadium ground-breaking

Pick ’15 wish

After essentially three years of discussion, study and controversy, Colorado State president Tony Frank made the recommendation to move forward with the on-campus stadium and eventually abandon Hughes Stadium. After several years of implying -- and even saying -- that Hughes Stadium, opened in 1968, was inadequate, CSU needs to move forward or it could mitigate the football program's momentum. As of now, the new stadium is tentatively slated to be ready for the 2017 season.

Bowling Buffaloes

Pick ’15 wish

D.D. Goodson, left, and Tyler McCulloch felt, again, the disappointment of a lost CU season. The Buffs finished 2-10 overall and 0-9 in the Pac-12. (Cliff Grassmick, Daily Camera)

It has been a long time. Colorado has not played in the postseason since 2007, when a Dan Hawkins-coached team faced Nick Saban's first Alabama team in the Independence Bowl in Shreveport, La. After spotting Alabama a 20-point first-quarter lead, CU played well. The Buffs lost, 30-24. To become bowl eligible in 2015, Colorado must show a huge improvement. But CU fans can be encouraged by all the close games against Pac-12 teams in 2014 (including double-overtime losses to California and UCLA) with a Buffs lineup filled with sophomores.

Rapids roster upgrade

Pick ’15 wish

With Edson Buddle gone, the Rapids need a target striker. And the’ve said they’re prepared to spend big on another attacking player in 2015. But they also need depth in central defense. Injuries to Shane O’Neill, Drew Moor and Jared Watts, coupled with a lack of MLS-ready depth behind them, played big roles in the club’s 2014 demise. To that end, the Rapids have brought in sporting director Padraig Smith, who plans to use analytics to help the club identify potential acquisitions.

New Rapids jersey sponsor (that pays)

Pick ’15 wish

The announcement of Ciao Telecom as the Rapids first front-of-jersey sponsor in May was a celebrated moment in franchise history. By October, the Rapids had filed a lawsuit alleging they were owed nearly a half-million dollars in unpaid sponsorship fees. With both sides mum, locked in ongoing litigation, it appears the Rapids will soon be on the hunt for a new front-of-jersey sponsor. And if that doesn’t happen, they could always bring back Rapidman.

Storybook World Ski Championships

Pick ’15 wish

Beaver Creek has hosted the World Ski Championships twice before, but never with two hometown favorites being strong medal contenders. Here's hoping Lindsey Vonn can come back from the knee injury that kept her out of the Sochi Olympics and win medals in downhill and super-G, and that Mikaela Shiffrin can win multiple medals on a mountain four miles from her home. It would be great to see Ted Ligety defend his gold medal in giant slalom, and to see 37-year-old Bode Miller be the king of the mountain one more time. February will be interesting.
